## Purpose

This proof-of-work case study shows how upstream healthcare workflow breakdowns can become downstream claim denials. The goal is to demonstrate practical revenue cycle thinking by tracing denials back to intake, eligibility verification, authorization review, documentation readiness, coding alignment, and follow-up processes.

## Core Analyst Insight

Denials often appear in billing, but many begin earlier in the workflow.

A denial is not always the first problem. It is often the final signal that an earlier workflow checkpoint failed.

## Problem

Healthcare organizations may treat denials as isolated billing issues after the claim has already been rejected or denied. That creates a reactive workflow where staff spend time correcting preventable problems instead of identifying why the denial happened in the first place.

Common upstream breakdowns include:

- incomplete intake information
- inactive or unverified eligibility
- incorrect payer or plan selection
- missed authorization requirements
- incomplete documentation
- unclear department handoffs
- delayed follow-up after payer response

## Root-Cause Breakdown

| Upstream Breakdown | Where It Starts | Downstream Denial Risk | Prevention Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Eligibility not verified | Patient access or registration | Coverage denial or claim rejection | Confirm active coverage for date of service |
| Wrong payer selected | Registration or payer setup | Claim routes to incorrect payer | Validate payer, plan type, and member ID |
| Authorization missed | Scheduling or authorization review | Missing authorization denial | Check payer rules before appointment confirmation |
| Documentation incomplete | Clinical documentation | Medical necessity denial | Use documentation readiness checklist |
| Coding does not match documentation | Coding and billing | Coding-related denial or rework | Review diagnosis, procedure, and documentation alignment |
| Follow-up delayed | A/R or denial follow-up | Aging claim balance and delayed reimbursement | Use priority follow-up queue by payer, balance, and deadline |
| Handoff unclear | Cross-department workflow | Duplicate work, missed task, delayed correction | Assign owner, next action, and due date |

## Operational Impact

When denial prevention is weak, the organization may experience:

- delayed reimbursement
- increased A/R days
- higher staff rework volume
- preventable claim corrections
- patient billing confusion
- reduced clean claim performance
- weaker trust between departments
- less visibility into where the workflow actually broke

## Prevention Strategy

A stronger denial prevention workflow should move key checks earlier in the revenue cycle.

Recommended checkpoints:

- verify active eligibility before service
- confirm payer and plan details
- identify referral or authorization requirements
- confirm documentation readiness before claim submission
- validate coding and documentation alignment
- route unresolved items to a clear owner
- track denial reasons by root cause, not only by payer response
- create a feedback loop from denial follow-up back to patient access, documentation, and billing teams

## Metrics to Track

| Metric | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| Eligibility-related denial rate | Whether front-end verification is protecting claim readiness |
| Authorization-related denial rate | Whether payer rule checks are happening before service |
| Clean claim rate | Whether claims are being submitted accurately the first time |
| A/R days | Whether delays are increasing reimbursement timelines |
| Rework volume | Whether preventable corrections are consuming staff time |
| Denial rate by root cause | Whether the organization understands where denials begin |
| Financial clearance completion rate | Whether accounts are ready before service occurs |

## Analyst Recommendation

Denial prevention should be treated as a workflow design issue, not only a billing correction task. The strongest improvement opportunity is to create earlier checkpoints that catch eligibility, authorization, documentation, coding, and handoff issues before the claim reaches the payer.
